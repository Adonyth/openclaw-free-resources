# 5 Claude Prompts for Academic Research

These prompts work with Claude (claude.ai), ChatGPT, or any capable LLM. Copy, customize the bracketed fields, and paste directly into the chat.

---

## Prompt 1: Literature Review Synthesis

**What it does:** Synthesizes a set of sources you've already read into a coherent narrative — saves hours of staring at a blank page.

**The Prompt:**

```
I'm writing a literature review on [topic] for a [undergraduate thesis / master's thesis / journal article / dissertation].

I'll paste summaries or key points from [number] sources below. For each one, I've noted the author, year, and main argument.

[Paste your source summaries here]

Please synthesize these into a cohesive 3–4 paragraph literature review narrative that:
- Groups sources thematically rather than listing them one by one
- Identifies agreements, contradictions, and gaps in the literature
- Uses an academic tone appropriate for [your field]
- Ends with a sentence that positions my own research in relation to these works

Do not fabricate citations or add sources I haven't listed.
```

**Example use case:** You've read 8 papers on remote work productivity. Paste their abstracts + your notes, and Claude drafts a literature review section that flows like you wrote it yourself.

## Prompt 2: Research Question Sharpener

**What it does:** Takes a vague research interest and helps you narrow it into a focused, defensible research question.

**The Prompt:**

```
I'm developing a research question for a [type of paper/project] in [field or discipline].

My broad topic is: [describe your area of interest in 1–2 sentences]

My current draft research question is: "[paste your draft question, or write 'I don't have one yet']"

Please help me sharpen this into a strong research question by:
1. Identifying what's too broad, too narrow, or unclear in my current framing
2. Offering 3 alternative versions of the question at different scopes (broad, medium, narrow)
3. For each version, briefly note what methodology it would imply (qualitative, quantitative, mixed, theoretical)
4. Recommending which version is most feasible for a [semester-long project / dissertation / etc.] and explaining why
```

**Example use case:** You want to study "social media and mental health" but your advisor says it's too broad. This prompt gives you 3 scoped alternatives with a clear rationale for choosing one.

## Prompt 3: Methodology Explainer & Critique

**What it does:** Explains a methodology in plain language, then stress-tests your plan against common objections — perfect for before a committee meeting or proposal defense.

**The Prompt:**

```
I'm using [methodology — e.g., semi-structured interviews, regression analysis, grounded theory, systematic review] for my research on [topic].

My current plan is: [describe your data collection, sample, and analysis approach in 3–5 sentences]

Please do three things:
1. Explain this methodology in plain language as if I needed to justify it to a skeptical committee member who prefers [alternative methodology]
2. List the 3 most likely critiques a reviewer would raise about my methodological choices
3. For each critique, give me a 2–3 sentence rebuttal I could use in my proposal or defense

Keep the tone academic but clear.
```

**Example use case:** You're doing qualitative interviews for a psychology study and your committee leans quantitative. This prompt preps you to defend your approach confidently.

## Prompt 4: Abstract Drafting & Polishing

**What it does:** Drafts or rewrites an abstract that hits all the required elements — background, gap, purpose, method, findings, implications — without sounding like a checklist.

**The Prompt:**

```
Please write an abstract for my [paper / thesis / conference submission] using the information below.

- **Field:** [your discipline]
- **Topic:** [one sentence]
- **Problem or gap addressed:** [what's missing in the literature that you tackle]
- **Research question or objective:** [your RQ]
- **Method:** [brief description]
- **Key findings or argument:** [what you found or claim]
- **Significance/implications:** [why it matters]
- **Word limit:** [150 / 250 / 300 words — check your target journal or conference]

Write the abstract as a single paragraph in [past / present] tense. Use the conventions of [APA / ACS / MLA / Chicago / your field's standard]. Do not use first person unless I specify.
```

**Example use case:** You've finished your paper but keep rewriting the abstract. Paste your details, get a solid draft in 30 seconds, then refine from there instead of from scratch.

## Prompt 5: Source Evaluation & Gap Identification

**What it does:** Given a set of sources, identifies what perspectives, time periods, or methodologies are missing — helps you find exactly what else you need to read.

**The Prompt:**

```
I'm researching [topic] and I've collected the following sources so far. I'll list each with: author, year, methodology, and main argument/finding.

[List your sources here in any format]

Based on this list, please:
1. Identify any obvious gaps — perspectives, populations, time periods, geographies, or methodologies that are underrepresented
2. Suggest 3–5 specific types of sources I should look for to fill those gaps (e.g., "a longitudinal study examining X in a non-Western context")
3. Flag any sources that might be methodologically weak or that reviewers would question, and explain why
4. Give me 3 search terms or Boolean search strings I could use in Google Scholar or a library database to find the missing sources

Do not invent specific papers or authors. Focus on the types and characteristics of sources I need.
```

**Example use case:** You have 12 sources but your literature review feels one-sided. This prompt audits your reading list and gives you a targeted search strategy for what's missing.

---

## Tips for Better Results

- **Be specific.** The more context you give (field, level, audience, word count), the better the output.
- **Iterate.** Paste Claude's output back and say "make it more concise" or "adjust the tone for a STEM audience."
- **Don't skip the 'do not fabricate' instruction.** LLMs can hallucinate citations. Always verify any reference before using it.
